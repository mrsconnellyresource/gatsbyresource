Jay Gatsby ▼

The enigmatic millionaire, known for his lavish parties and a singular, relentless pursuit of the past.

"Can't repeat the past? Why of course you can!"

**Analysis Idea:** Reveals Gatsby's boundless optimism, his denial of reality, and the central conflict of his ambition.

"He was an elegant roughneck, a year or two over thirty, whose elaborate formality of speech just missed being absurd." - Nick Carraway

**Analysis Idea:** Nick's initial perception of Gatsby, highlighting his mysterious charm blended with an underlying lack of refinement.

"He had an extraordinary gift for hope, a romantic readiness such as I have never found in any other person and which it is not likely I shall ever find again." - Nick Carraway

**Analysis Idea:** Emphasizes Gatsby's defining characteristic – his unwavering optimism and capacity for dreams, a quality Nick admires despite Gatsby's flaws.

"His parents were shiftless and unsuccessful farm people—his imagination had never really accepted them as his parents at all." - Narrator

**Analysis Idea:** This quote reveals Gatsby's true origins and his early rejection of his humble beginnings, which fueled his desire to reinvent himself.

Daisy Buchanan ▼

The object of Gatsby's affection, a beautiful and alluring woman whose voice is "full of money."

"Her voice compelled me forward breathlessly as if I had been toiling for it all my life. . . . it was a voice whose thrilling perfunctoriness committed itself to the glory of the next hour." - Nick Carraway

**Analysis Idea:** Nick's mesmerized description of Daisy's voice, hinting at its alluring yet superficial quality and her focus on the immediate rather than the profound.

"I hope she'll be a fool—that's the best thing a girl can be in this world, a beautiful little fool."

**Analysis Idea:** Reveals Daisy's cynical understanding of her social position and the perceived advantages of ignorance for women in her era.

"They were careless people, Tom and Daisy—they smashed up things and creatures and then retreated back into their money or their vast carelessness, or whatever it was that kept them together, and let other people clean up the mess they had made." - Nick Carraway

**Analysis Idea:** Nick's final, damning judgment of Daisy and Tom, highlighting their irresponsibility, privilege, and ultimate moral cowardice.

Tom Buchanan ▼

Daisy's immensely wealthy and arrogant husband, a former football star with a strong sense of entitlement and prejudice.

"He had changed since his New Haven years. Now he was a sturdy straw-haired man of thirty with a rather hard mouth and a supercilious manner." - Nick Carraway

**Analysis Idea:** Nick's introduction of Tom, immediately establishing his imposing physical presence and arrogant demeanor.

"The idea is if we don’t look out the white race will be—will be utterly submerged. It’s all scientific stuff; it’s been proved."

**Analysis Idea:** Exposes Tom's deep-seated racism, insecurity, and his reliance on pseudo-intellectualism to justify his prejudices.

"And what’s more, I love Daisy too. Once in a while I go off on a spree and make a fool of myself, but I always come back, and in my heart I love her all the time."

**Analysis Idea:** Reveals Tom's hypocritical moral code and his possessive view of Daisy, despite his own infidelity.

Nick Carraway ▼

The novel's narrator, a thoughtful and observant young man who moves to West Egg and becomes entangled in the lives of the wealthy.

"In consequence I’m inclined to reserve all judgments, a habit that has opened up many curious natures to me and also made me the victim of not a few veteran bores."

**Analysis Idea:** Nick's self-introduction, establishing his role as an observer and confidant, explaining why he is privy to so much information.

"I was within and without, simultaneously enchanted and repelled by the inexhaustible variety of life."

**Analysis Idea:** Captures Nick's dual perspective throughout the novel – fascinated by the glamour but ultimately disgusted by the moral decay of the wealthy.

"So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past." - Narrator (Nick)

**Analysis Idea:** The novel's famous closing line, reflecting on humanity's struggle against the relentless pull of history and the elusive nature of the American Dream, as observed by Nick.

Myrtle Wilson ▼

Tom Buchanan's vivacious and ambitious mistress, who longs to climb the social ladder.

"She was in the middle thirties, and faintly stout, but she carried her flesh sensuously as some women can. Her face, above a spotted dress of dark blue crepe-de-chine, contained no gleam of beauty, but there was an immediately perceptible vitality about her." - Nick Carraway

**Analysis Idea:** Nick's initial description of Myrtle, emphasizing her raw vitality and earthy sensuality, contrasting with Daisy's ethereal beauty.

"I told that boy about the ice." She turned to Tom, expecting a suggestion. "This is a terrible room," she said. "It's always the way."

**Analysis Idea:** Shows Myrtle's assertive and demanding nature, particularly when she is in a situation where she feels she has power (like in her apartment with Tom).

"The only *crazy* thing I was in was when I married him. I knew right away I made a mistake."

**Analysis Idea:** Reveals Myrtle's dissatisfaction with her marriage to George Wilson and her aspirations for a more glamorous, wealthy life.

Other Characters & Themes ▼

Explore quotes related to other significant figures or overarching themes like the American Dream, Social Class, and Illusion vs. Reality.

"Gatsby believed in the green light, the orgastic future that year by year recedes before us. It eluded us then, but that’s no matter—tomorrow we will run faster, stretch out our arms farther. . . ." - Nick Carraway (regarding American Dream)

**Analysis Idea:** Connects Gatsby's personal dream to the broader, elusive American Dream, highlighting its promise and its ultimate unattainability.
